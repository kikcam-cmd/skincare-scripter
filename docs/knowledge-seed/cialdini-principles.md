# Cialdini's Seven Principles of Influence

Tight reference of Robert Cialdini's persuasion principles (the six from *Influence* plus Unity from the 2021 revision), reframed for short-form video script-gen grounding. Each principle is a citeable unit. Source-label suggestion when uploading: `Cialdini - Principles`.

## Reciprocity

People feel obligated to return value when they receive it first. Even small unsolicited concessions activate the indebting force, and the obligation often outweighs the original gift. The lever is the order of operations: give first, ask second.

TikTok application: lead with concrete value (a tested technique, a controversial cost comparison, a free objection-killer tip) *before* the product mention or CTA. The viewer has already received something useful by the time they're asked to click.

## Commitment and Consistency

Once people take a small public stance, they pressure themselves to act consistently with it. The lever is the initial micro-yes — a question they silently nod to, a problem they admit having. Tiny commitments compound into larger ones along the same identity line.

TikTok application: open with a question or claim that pulls a silent agreement ("if you've ever wasted money on a lip plumper that just burned…", "raise your hand if you've watched the same review three times before buying"). The rest of the script earns its place by being consistent with the viewer's now-stated identity.

## Social Proof

People look to similar others to decide what to do, especially under uncertainty or in unfamiliar situations. The lever is *similarity*, not authority — "people like you do X" beats "everyone does X". Visible consensus is most powerful when it comes from the viewer's reference group.

TikTok application: show comment overlays from peer creators, sell-out counts, FYP-chain references ("everyone keeps stitching this one"), Reddit/Pinterest screenshots. The reference group must match the target demo — Gen-Z testimonials for Gen-Z scripts, anti-aging community for 40+ scripts.

## Authority

People defer to credentialed expertise — even in domains adjacent to the credential — because evaluating expertise is harder than borrowing it. Titles, uniforms, specialized vocabulary, and institutional context activate compliance with low cognitive cost.

TikTok application: lead with credentialing ("as a nurse / esthetician / dermatologist…") OR borrow institutional authority ("the same compound used in medical-grade procedures", "FDA filed under…"). Stack credentials sparingly — one strong credential beats three weak ones, and visible credentials beat claimed ones.

## Liking

People comply more with those they like — and likability comes from similarity, sincere compliments, cooperative framing, and shared adversaries. The lever is the parasocial friend tone: *"I'm telling you what I'd tell my best friend"*.

TikTok application: car-confessional or bedroom-selfie energy weaponizes liking. Mirror the viewer's vocabulary, complain about the same things they complain about, break the polished-presenter frame. UGC aesthetic codes as trustworthy because it codes as a friend, not a salesperson.

## Scarcity

Things that are rare, restricted, or about to disappear are perceived as more valuable. The lever combines loss aversion (avoiding a missed opportunity) with the cue that competitors want it too. Scarcity is most credible when *specific*.

TikTok application: specific scarcity reads as reportage ("sold out four times in two months", "shipping pushed back twice", "the last batch", "ends Sunday at midnight"). Vague scarcity ("limited time!", "while supplies last!") reads as marketing and gets ignored. Pair with social proof for compounding effect.

## Unity

The strongest compliance lever is shared identity — *we*, not *they*. Unity goes deeper than liking because it implies common selfhood, not just similarity. People act in the interests of their in-group as if those interests were their own.

TikTok application: unity-framing is the difference between *"Korean girls already know about this"* (exclusion, weaker) and *"us girls born before the 2000s are all dealing with this volume loss"* (inclusion, stronger). The viewer's identity becomes the script's POV — the creator speaks as a member of the group, not a guide to it.

---

## Application notes for script-gen

- These are the source of *cross-product* persuasion patterns. Per-video corpus chunks teach what a specific creator did; these teach the underlying lever.
- A single hook usually combines two or three principles (e.g. a nurse-creator authority hook with social-proof comment overlays + sell-out scarcity).
- When the corpus for a product is saturated with one tactic, lifting an under-used principle from this list is often the strongest move to diversify angles.
- Authority and Unity travel poorly across cultures; what reads as authority signaling in one demo can read as gatekeeping in another. Match the principle to the creator's actual identity.
